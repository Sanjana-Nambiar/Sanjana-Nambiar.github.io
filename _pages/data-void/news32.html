Skill Atrophy Thresholds

A longitudinal analysis of how copilot reliance affects error detection, retention, and independent problem-solving. longitudinal pre-registered

AI copilots can speed up work without making it feel easier and a research team at the Center for Adaptive Work Systems argues that this mismatch hides a measurable phenomenon: skill atrophy thresholds, where reliance crosses a point beyond which error-detection and retention decline even as immediate task throughput improves.

Key framing: This report does not claim “AI makes people stupid.” It claims a narrower effect: assistance can reduce the need to perform internal checks, and that reduction compounds over time.
Working Definition
A skill atrophy threshold is the minimum sustained exposure to copilot assistance after which a user’s independent performance (without assistance) declines on the same task family, relative to baseline.
Study design

96 participants were tracked over 28 days across three task families: debugging, analytic writing, and spreadsheet modeling. Participants were randomly assigned to one of three assistance conditions: low, moderate, or high.

The “threshold” is not a single number

The researchers argue against a universal threshold, proposing instead a family of thresholds by task type and user expertise. However, they report a consistent pattern: atrophy effects accelerate when a user’s workflow shifts from verification-first to acceptance-first behavior.

“The assistant didn’t remove the skill. It removed the need to exercise the skill — and that’s enough to change the baseline.” — Dr. Rowan Keats, Study Lead

What changes first

Notably, the first measurable decline was not output quality during assisted work — it was self-correction: participants became slower to notice contradictions, edge-case failures, and small logic gaps when the assistant was unavailable.

Limitations

  • Task bounded: results may not generalize beyond the studied task families.
  • Tool effects: specific UI designs can amplify or dampen atrophy.
  • Short horizon: 28 days suggests early dynamics; long-term adaptation may differ.
