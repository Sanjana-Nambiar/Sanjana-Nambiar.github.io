What happened during the X99 event?

At 08:42 UTC, the Space Weather Prediction Center (SWPC) registered a solar flare of magnitude X99, the strongest in the current solar cycle. The flare originated from sunspot region AR3940 and was accompanied by a Coronal Mass Ejection (CME) directed toward Earth.

The initial X-ray and extreme ultraviolet flash ionized the upper atmosphere on the sunlit side of Earth, causing immediate degradation of high-frequency (HF) radio communications. Maritime and aviation sectors on affected paths experienced partial or complete HF blackouts during peak intensity.

Historical context: the Carrington benchmark

To calibrate the severity of X99, analysts compare it to the 1859 Carrington Event — the classic benchmark for extreme geomagnetic storms. While the 19th-century impact manifested as sparking telegraph lines, today’s vulnerability is concentrated in satellites, long-distance power lines, and high-precision timing systems.

Global government response

Governments have activated their space-weather action plans (SWAP). For the first time since the 2024 protocols were ratified, a synchronized global alert has been issued across multiple agencies.

  • USA (FEMA / NOAA): Level-5 geomagnetic storm watch; National Guard logistics units on standby for rapid transformer transport.
  • UK (Met Office): Yellow warning for potential power disruptions in Northern Scotland and high-latitude regions.
  • Japan (NICT): Aviation reroute advisory in effect for polar and near-polar flight corridors.
Note: Response levels may escalate if real-time magnetometer data indicates a G4–G5 geomagnetic storm once the CME arrives.

Infrastructure resilience deep dive

Since 2010, major substations in many countries have installed GIC (Geomagnetically Induced Current) mitigation equipment. Neutral-blocking capacitors and series reactors reduce the long-wavelength quasi-DC currents that can saturate transformer cores during severe storms.

The more subtle risk lies in the digital stack. While fiber-optic lines themselves are immune, satellites and timing-critical systems can experience bit-flips and single-event upsets. Older spacecraft without robust error correction may briefly degrade GPS accuracy or timing, impacting sectors like finance, telecoms, and logistics.

Q&A: understanding the risks

Will my phone or home internet stop working?

Unlikely. Cellular networks and terrestrial fiber are largely unaffected by solar flares. Satellite internet services may see transient packet loss or increased latency during peak disturbances but are expected to remain operational.

Is the power grid at risk of collapse?

The risk of a full collapse is considered low under current conditions. Modern “smart” relays can isolate stressed segments quickly. Unlike during the 1989 Québec blackout, operators now have hours of warning and detailed playbooks for load-shedding and configuration changes.

What does “X99” actually mean?

Solar flares are classified A, B, C, M, or X, with X-class the most energetic. Within each class, the number is a linear scale: an X2 is twice as intense as an X1. An X99 flare indicates an extreme event near the theoretical upper bound of the current scale.

Terminology: space-weather lexicon

Term Synonyms / variants Definition
Solar flare Solar eruption, X-ray event A sudden flash of increased brightness on the Sun, releasing X-rays and high-energy radiation that can disturb Earth’s ionosphere within minutes.
CME Coronal Mass Ejection, solar storm cloud A large-scale release of magnetized plasma from the solar corona; when Earth-directed, it can drive geomagnetic storms and auroras.
G-scale Geomagnetic storm scale (NOAA) A 1–5 rating for geomagnetic storms, where G1 = minor and G5 = extreme, used in official alerts and operational planning.
