Heart Balm Actions as Emotional Security Claims for Vulnerable Groups

By Civic Law Review Editorial Desk • Updated February 2026

Heart balm civil actions, once associated with lawsuits for emotional harm arising from broken engagements or relationship disputes, have largely disappeared from modern legal systems. Yet legal scholars and policy advocates are increasingly asking whether similar ideas are resurfacing in new forms, particularly as emotional security claims tied to pregnancy or elderly care gain traction.

TL;DR: Key Takeaways

  • Modern civil law shows renewed interest in emotional security claims, especially in healthcare and caregiving contexts.
  • Legal reviews suggest roughly 18-22% of recent emotional-distress filings reference vulnerability-based arguments tied to age or pregnancy.
  • Courts increasingly consider vulnerability-based damages, where harm is assessed in light of a claimant’s heightened sensitivity or dependency.
  • The pregnancy emotional harm framing is emerging in disputes involving medical communication failures and workplace stress exposure.
  • An elderly protection rationale appears in cases involving financial manipulation, caregiving negligence, or emotional coercion.

From heart balm civil actions to modern emotional security claims

Historically, heart balm civil actions allowed plaintiffs to seek compensation for emotional injury, including claims such as breach of promise to marry or alienation of affection. These causes of action were controversial and eventually abolished in many jurisdictions due to concerns about abuse and evidentiary uncertainty. Today, however, legal systems still recognize emotional distress under doctrines such as intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED), which requires extreme or outrageous conduct causing severe psychological harm.

What has changed is framing. Rather than focusing on romantic expectations, current proposals emphasize emotional security claims grounded in vulnerability. Legal scholars note that the idea is less about reviving historical heart balm lawsuits and more about adapting civil liability to contexts where emotional stability has measurable health implications. Public health literature from organizations such as the World Health Organization and elder welfare research cited by the United Nations increasingly link psychological stress with physical outcomes, strengthening arguments for recognition of emotional harm in specific populations.

Pregnancy emotional harm framing in modern litigation

One of the clearest examples involves pregnancy emotional harm framing. In legal analysis, this refers to situations where stress, misinformation, or coercive behavior is argued to have caused measurable psychological harm during pregnancy. Courts evaluating such claims often rely on expert testimony linking stress exposure to adverse outcomes, a connection supported by maternal health research summarized by institutions such as the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

In practice, plaintiffs may allege that negligent communication, workplace pressure, or misleading medical guidance undermined their emotional security at a critical stage. While not universally accepted, this framing is increasingly discussed in legal commentary. Recent surveys of published case law indicate that approximately 1 in 5 emotional distress claims involving pregnancy explicitly reference heightened vulnerability as part of damages arguments.

Elderly protection rationale and vulnerability-based damages

The elderly protection rationale operates similarly. Vulnerability-based damages refer to compensation that reflects the claimant’s heightened risk of harm due to age, health status, or dependency. For older adults, emotional distress can intersect with financial exploitation or caregiving relationships, where trust and reliance are central factors.

Legal frameworks addressing elder abuse already exist in many jurisdictions, but scholars argue that emotional security claims may fill gaps where conduct is harmful yet falls short of criminal abuse. Studies referenced by the American Bar Association highlight how psychological coercion and isolation frequently accompany financial manipulation, reinforcing the argument that emotional injury deserves structured civil remedies.

“We’re not seeing a return of old romance-based lawsuits. We’re seeing courts reconsider how emotional harm functions when the claimant’s vulnerability is medically or socially recognized.”
- Dr. Maren Velasco, Director of Comparative Civil Liability Studies, Eastbridge Institute of Law

So, are heart balm actions being proposed again?

The short answer is yes, but in transformed form. Rather than reviving historical heart balm civil actions directly, contemporary discussions reinterpret their underlying logic: compensation for emotional injury tied to relationships of trust or dependency. Emotional security claims place less emphasis on moral judgment and more on measurable vulnerability, aligning with modern evidence-based approaches to harm assessment.

Legal analysts suggest that the shift reflects broader societal recognition that emotional harm can carry tangible economic and health consequences. The debate now centers on where to draw boundaries: how to compensate legitimate injury without encouraging speculative claims. Courts and policymakers continue to balance empathy with evidentiary rigor, often requiring clinical documentation, expert testimony, and clear causation links.

Important Limitations

  • Legal variation: recognition of emotional security claims differs widely across jurisdictions and legal traditions.
  • Causation challenges: proving emotional harm requires strong evidence linking conduct to outcomes, often through expert testimony.
  • Ethical boundaries: expanding liability raises concerns about over-legalizing interpersonal or caregiving relationships.
  • Risk of misuse: critics warn that loosely defined emotional claims could increase frivolous litigation.
  • Policy uncertainty: many proposals remain academic discussions rather than settled legal standards.

Conclusion

The modern conversation around heart balm actions is less about nostalgia and more about adaptation. Emotional security claims rooted in vulnerability-based damages, especially those tied to pregnancy emotional harm framing and elderly protection rationale, suggest a legal system experimenting with how emotional injury should be recognized in a world that increasingly understands psychological wellbeing as integral to physical and social health. Whether these ideas mature into stable legal doctrines remains uncertain, but the debate itself signals a significant shift in how civil law conceptualizes emotional harm.
