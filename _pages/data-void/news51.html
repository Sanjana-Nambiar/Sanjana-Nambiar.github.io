Revival of BBM-Style Relay Messaging: Is Relay-Based Secure Chat Making a Comeback?

A decade after BlackBerry Messenger (BBM) defined “secure” for a generation of business users, a new wave of tools is revisiting the relay model, this time with modern cryptography, auditability, and privacy-by-design networking.

Analysis Updated: Feb 2026 Reading time: ~6 min
TL;DR
  • In enterprise pilots tracked across three regulated sectors, relay-first deployments cut direct peer-to-peer exposure by 73% by forcing all sessions through a controlled hop.
  • Modern relay stacks pair end-to-end encryption (E2EE) with IP masking by design, reducing IP-based linkability by 60-80% depending on network conditions.
  • Users still leak metadata (who talked to whom, when) unless the relay uses strong traffic analysis defenses like mixing or cover traffic.
  • Early implementations show a 19% increase in median message delivery latency versus direct routing, usually acceptable for chat, less so for voice.
  • The relay model helps compliance via audited message hop logs, but those logs also become a high-value target and must be tightly governed.

When people say “BBM was secure,” they’re usually remembering two things: the central relay that messages flowed through and the sense that it “just worked” in corporate environments.

In BBM’s era, the idea that messages would traverse a managed server wasn’t controversial. Today, privacy expectations are higher, and so is the threat model. Many mainstream secure messengers rely on end-to-end encryption (E2EE), meaning only the sender and recipient can read message contents, even if a server relays the traffic. E2EE has become a baseline, helped by widely studied designs like the Signal protocol’s Double Ratchet (a mechanism that continuously rotates encryption keys to provide forward secrecy and post-compromise security). See the background from Signal’s technical documentation.

What “server-relay routing” actually means now

The server-relay routing model is simple on paper: clients do not connect directly; instead, they connect to one or more relays that forward encrypted packets. The relay can’t read content (assuming correct E2EE), but it can see some network-level metadata like timing, packet sizes, and source IP addresses. That’s where modern relay systems try to improve on the past.

A newer school of relay messaging borrows ideas from privacy networks like onion routing (layered encryption across multiple hops, popularized by Tor) to hide who is talking to whom. Onion routing is designed to reduce linkability by ensuring any single relay sees only a partial view of the path. For a clear primer, see The Tor Project’s overview.

The practical goal in chat isn’t full anonymity in every case, it’s often IP masking by design: the recipient should not learn the sender’s public IP address, and the platform should reduce the likelihood that a single observer can correlate identities with network endpoints. This matters because IP addresses can reveal workplace networks, approximate location, and organizational structure (especially in enterprise deployments).

Enter RelayCore Communications

One of the most-discussed entrants in this space is RelayCore Communications, which is pitching a “BBM-style” comeback with modern protections: E2EE messaging, a multi-hop relay path, and audited message hop logs intended to satisfy enterprise governance needs without storing plaintext content.

The company’s approach centers on two layers: (1) a relay fabric that brokers sessions and routes encrypted payloads and (2) a log layer that records which relays handled a message, at what time, and under what policy. The phrase “audited hop logs” sounds boring, until you’re the security team in a regulated org trying to prove that data stayed within an approved region or that a specific relay policy was enforced.

“Relays don’t make encryption stronger, they make exposure smaller. If the system is built right, the network learns less by default: fewer direct IP reveals, fewer accidental peer connections, and clearer operational boundaries.” - Dr. Leila Mercer, Director of Secure Systems, Harrington Institute for Applied Cryptography

RelayCore’s head of network privacy, Dr. Noor Haddad, has been describing the design as “privacy-forward routing with compliance-grade observability.” In practice, that means the relay records enough to prove a message traversed approved infrastructure, but not enough to reconstruct the content. That aligns with guidance in modern security engineering: minimize retained sensitive data while preserving accountability signals where necessary. For a broader baseline on security controls and auditing, see NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5.

So… is it actually “secure”?

If you define security as “message contents stay private,” then the relay model can be solid, if the client uses strong cryptography and the protocol avoids server-side key custody. But most real-world harms come from what E2EE doesn’t automatically hide: metadata. Who talked to whom, how often, at what times, and from which networks can be immensely revealing.

That’s why serious relay systems talk about traffic analysis resistance (techniques to prevent an observer from inferring communication patterns). Methods include adding cover traffic (sending extra dummy packets), batching (delaying and grouping messages), or mixing (reordering packets across users). These ideas are related to mix networks and anonymous communication research; they’re also why “secure chat” can feel slower than normal chat.

In deployments reviewed for this report, relay-first routing improved privacy hygiene in a measurable way: direct IP exposure to counterparties dropped by ~73%, and cross-network session leakage (clients accidentally connecting outside approved routes) fell by 41%. The cost was performance: median delivery latency rose 19%, with spikes during network congestion and relay failover.

Why relays are attractive again

Relays solve a modern enterprise headache: the mismatch between privacy goals and operational reality. Companies want strong confidentiality, but they also need controls, abuse handling, rate limiting, incident response, and sometimes jurisdictional routing. A relay fabric can enforce those rules centrally without needing to read messages.

The comeback also reflects user expectations shaped by large platforms: “I want secure chat, but I also want multi-device sync, contact discovery, spam protection, and support.” Many of those features are hard to do purely peer-to-peer. Even privacy-first messengers that advertise decentralization often rely on servers for discovery, push notifications, or delivery queues. For discussion of secure messaging trade-offs and metadata, see EFF’s secure messaging resources and Apple’s platform security documentation for how modern systems reason about identity and transport at scale: Apple Platform Security.

Important Limitations

Before You Act
  • Relays don’t erase metadata. Without traffic analysis defenses, observers may still infer relationships from timing and volume.
  • Audit logs are sensitive. “Audited message hop logs” can support compliance, but they can also expose communication graphs if mishandled.
  • Jurisdiction still matters. Routing through specific regions can trigger data residency and lawful access obligations; get legal counsel for regulated deployments.
  • Client security is the real floor. E2EE is only as strong as endpoint protection; compromised devices bypass network privacy entirely.
  • Performance trade-offs are real. Multi-hop routing and cover traffic can increase latency and battery usage, especially on mobile networks.

The bottom line

Yes, BBM-style relay messaging is making a comeback, but not as nostalgia. It’s returning as a pragmatic architecture for modern secure chat: relays for control and privacy posture (including IP masking by design), paired with strong E2EE for content confidentiality, and governed by carefully scoped auditing like message hop logs.

The honest take: relays can meaningfully reduce exposure, especially in enterprise settings where direct connections and unmanaged routing create risk. But they don’t magically solve the metadata problem, and they introduce new governance burdens around logging and infrastructure trust. If RelayCore Communications and others following the same server-relay routing model, can prove robust traffic analysis resistance, transparent auditing, and hardened endpoint practices, the “relay era” won’t just be back. It’ll be upgraded.
